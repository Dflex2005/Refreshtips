Stress

Stress is the extra force or greater emphasis placed on a syllable when it is pronounced in order to make it prominent than the others.

Rule 1

If a bi-syllabic word has a prefix, the stress should be on the second syllable.
Examples:
disgrace /dis-GRACE/
apart /a-PART/
behind /be-HIND/

Rule 2

Nouns and adjectives with two syllables, the stress is usually on the first syllable.
Examples:
window /WIN-dow/
table /TA-ble/
clever /CLE-ver/
NOTE: there are some exceptions like extreme /ex-TREME/

Rule 3

Words that are both a noun and a verb, the noun has it stress on the first syllable and the verb has it stress on the second syllable.
Examples:
transport /TRANS-port/ (NOUN), /trans-PORT/(VERB)
produce /PRO-duce/ (NOUN), /pro-DUCE/ (VERB)

Rule 4

Verbs and prepositions with two syllables, the stress is usually on the second syllable.
EXamples:
export /ex-PORT/
aside /a-SIDE/

Rule 5

Compound adjectives and verbs. In most compound adjectives (a single adjective made of more than one word and often linked with a hyphen) and compound verbs (a multi-word verb that functions as a single verb) the stress is on the second word.
Examples:
understand /un-der-STAND/
old-fashioned /old-FA-shioned

Rule 6

Compound nouns(a noun made up of two or more existing words), the word stress is on the first noun.
Examples:
keyboard /KEY-board/
football /FOOT-ball/

Rule 7

Poly-syllabic words that ends in: ate, phy, cal, ium, cy, al, ent, ant, fy, ty and gy have their stress on the third syllable from the end of the word(it means you count backwards).
Examples:
matriculate /ma-TRI-cu-late/
photography /pho-TO-gra-phy/
geographical /ge-o-GRA-phi-cal/
equilibrium /e-QUI-lib-rium/
democracy /de-MO-cra-cy/
logical /LO-gi-cal/
environment /en-VI-ron-ment/
predominant /pre-DO-mi-nant/
crucify /CRU-ci-fy/
identity /i-DEN-ti-ty/
psychology /psy-CHO-lo-gy/
NOTE: some words that ends in "ium" are stress on the second syllable. Examples: condominum /con-do-MIN-ium/, pandominium /pan-do-MIN-ium/ etc.

Rule 8

Poly-syllabic words that ends in: ion, ic, ial and ian have their stress on the second syllable(backwards).
Examples:
education /e-du-CA-tion/
photographic /pho-to-GRA-phic/
testimonial /tes-ti-MO-nial/
politician /po-li-TI-cian/

Rule 9

Poly-syllabic words that ends in: ee, que and ere have their stress on the last syllable.
Examples:
commitee /com-mi-TEE/
communique /com-mu-ni-QUE/
interfere /in-ter-FERE/

Rule 10

Quatra-syllabic words(four syllable words) that ends in: ism, ble, ure, ry, tor, ize and ive have their stress on the first syllable.
Examples:
tribalism /TRI-ba-li-sm/
favourable /FA-vou-ra-ble/
infrastructure /IN-fra-struc-ture/
dormitory /DOR-mi-to-ry/
generator /GE-ne-ra-tor/
nationalize /NA-tion-al-ize/
decorative /DE-co-ra-tive/

concord

Concord is the agreement between subject and the verb.

Rule 1

Subject and verb concord
When the subject in a sentence is singular, the verb should also be singular.
For example,
She goes, not: She go.
Also, when the subject is plural, the verb should be plural.
For example
The boys run, not: The boys runs.

Rule 2

Indefinite pronoun concord.
When any of the following words are used, you should use a Singular verb Such words as; Everybody, everything, everyone, everywhere, no one, nothing, nobody, nowhere, something, someone, somebody, anyone, anything, anybody, anywhere and each. the next verb must be singular.
For example,
Everybody likes (not like) him.

Rule 3

Subject and Object concord.
When "everybody" or "everyone" is used, the object must be singular, not plural.
For example,
Everybody knows his or her age, not: Everybody knows their age.

Rule 4

Mandative subjunctive concord.
When prayer, suggestion, wish, demand, recommendation, or resolution is used in a sentence, the verb that follows must be plural, whether the subject is singular or plural.
For example,
It has been suggested that she go, not: goes away.
The board has Recommend that the manager resign, not: resigns.
I pray that God deliver me, not: God delivers.

Rule 5

A number of concord.
When "a number of concord" is use, a plural noun is use as well as a plural verb.
For example,
A number of girls are here.

Rule 6

The number of concord.
When "the number of concord" is use in a sentence, a plural noun and a singular verb is use.
For example,
The number of girls is (not are).

Rule 7

Many - a concord.
When "many - a" is used, the verb and the noun that follows must be Singular For example, Many a candidate (not candidates) speaks ( not speak) bad English expressions. Many a boy (not boys) is (not are) here.

Rule 8

A pair of concord.
When “a pair of” is used, the verb must be singular.
For example,
A pair of scissors lies (not lie) on the table.

Rule 9

Percentage concord.
When a percentage or fraction preceed a noun, the choice of the verb is determined by the kind of noun applied.
For example,
17% of the students have been promoted.
3/4 of the classroom have been painted.

Rule 10

The Principle of proximity.
This principle states that when there is a list of nouns or pronouns at the level of the subject, it is the nearest noun or pronoun to the position of the verb that will determine the choice of the verb.
For example,
If James fails his examination, his teachers, his parents, his friends, or John is to be blame.
If James fails his examination, his teachers, his parents, his friends, or I am to be blame.

Rule 11

National Concord.
National concord is also called collective noun concord. A plural or singulas verb goes with a collective noun.
For example,
Our group meet (not meets) ones in a week.However, in some situations, a singular verb goes with a collective noun. Here is the principle;
If the collective noun performs an action, a plural verb follows, but if not, a singular verb follows. For example: Our group is celebrating its twentieth anniversary today.

Rule 12

Parenthesis.
The parenthesis statement is an additional statement to what has already been said before.
Note A parenthetical statement should not be considered in choosing the verb that will follow.
For example,
The manager, not many of his workers, is (not are) here now. not many of his workers is just a parenthesis, therefore, the parenthesis should be ignored.
The manager is a singular noun, hence a singular verb.

Rule 13

More than concord.
When "more than" is used, the word or number that comes after more than will determine the next verb.
For example,
More than two pencils are ( not is) here.
More than one pencils is (not are) here.

Rule 14

Most or much concord.
When "most" is used, the verb will either be singular or plural, depending on whether the referent is a countable or uncountable noun.
For example,
Most of the girls are tall. Most of the time, David has (not have) always been there for her.
When "Much" is used in a statement, the verb to be used must be singular.
For example,
Much of the oil has (not have) been spilled.

Rule 15

All concord.
When "all" is used, it means either everything or all the people. When all means everything, the verb to be used should be Singular but when all means all the people, the verb to be used should be plural.
For example,
All are already seated in the church. “All” means all the people are already seated in the church hence a plural verb.
But; All is well with me. “All” means everything is well with me hence a singular verb.

When all but is used, the following verb should be plural.
For example,
All but samuel are (not is) in the house. That means, only samuel is absent.

Rule 16

Either or neither concord.
When either or neither joins two singular nouns together, the following verb should be Singular.
For example,
Either Bright or Favour knows (not know) me. Neither Emmanuel nor his friend was (not were) here.

But, when either or neither joins two subjects (one singular and the other plural), the verb should be chosen by considering the nearer of the two subjects.
For example,
Either Bright or his friends know (not knows) me.

These rules also apply, when “but or but even” joins two subjects.
For example,
Not Blessing but David speaks good English.

Rule 17

Relative Concord.
When who, whose, which and that refers to a previously mentioned noun or pronoun, such noun is a Relative noun.
For example,
One of the farmers who plant (not plants) on the farm has (not have) been asked to withdraw.

Rule 18

Double title subject concord.
When two subjects are joined together by “and” but the two subjects refer to only one person or thing, a singular verb should be used.
For example,
Our pastor and minister knows me.
but when "the" is applied it becomes two different subject(Our pastor and the minister).

Rule 19

Co-ordinate concord.
When two subjects are joined together by and, the verb to be used should be plural.
For example,
Samuel and David are (not is) here.

Rule 20

Accompaniment Concord.
When any of these following words are used, the subject of the clause would be the noun and pronoun that comes before the marker of accompaniment.
Words like as much as, alongside, as well as, together with, no less than, in association with, including, like, with, and in collaboration with, etc.
Example 1
Angel, as well as her friends, is ( not are) beautiful. "is" is used because Angel is a singular noun, so a singular verb is used.

Example 2
The little children alongside their parents are (not is) here. "are" is used because little children is a plural noun, so a plural verb is used.

Rule 21

Plural number concord.
When amount or unit is used in a statement, units such as ten thousand, three hundred, percent, twenty meters, five times, etc. The next verb must be singular.
For example.
Ten percent of my income goes to the church.

Rule 22

Mathematical facts.
When mathematical facts are used, such as subtraction, multiplication, addition, division, a singular or plural verb can be used when mathematical facts are used.
For example,
Two plus two is four (Correct).
Two plus two are four (Correct).

Rule 23

Each and each or one of concord.
When each appears in concord, a singular noun and a singular verb will be used.
For example,
Each girl (not girls) has a phone.

But, when "each of or one of" appears, the next noun should be plural but the next verb should be singular.
For example,
Each of the students (not student) stands (not stand) a good chance to win a scholarship.

Rule 24

Categorization concord.
When a collective name, denoting category (not a collective noun) is used, the verb to be used must be plural
Categories like: the rich, the successful, the talented, the helpless, etc.The verb used must be plural.
For example,
The young are (not is) faithful.

Rule 25

Every and plural number concord When every precedes a plural number, the next verb is plural.
For example,
Every ten cartons of noodles brought come (not comes) with a bonus of an extra carton.

But when “every” is used without any plural number, the verb is singular.
For example,
Every boy (not boys) likes football.

When and joins two or more subjects with every or each, the verb should be singular.
For example,
Every student and teacher comes ( not come) to school early.

Rule 26

Uncountable nouns of concord.
Uncountable nouns like: water, information, equipment. plural verb is used.
For example,
Aircraft make (not makes) traveling easier.

Rule 27

Pluralia tantums.
Pluralia tantum are nouns that come in plural forms.
Some of these words have final “s”, while some do not. However, whenever any of the following forms appear, it must be followed by a singular verb.
Words like: Economics, snakes and ladders, measles, etc.
For example,
The means of transportion hastens (not hasten) traveling.
